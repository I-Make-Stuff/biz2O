# Example Business: The Constitutional Platform Company

This document provides a concrete example of a business created,
governed, and operated using the Constitutional Governance Framework.

The purpose of this example is to:
- demonstrate real-world adoption
- show how tokens, governance, and distributions interact
- illustrate safe early-stage collaboration
- serve as a reference for new adopters

This is an illustrative example, not a prescription.

---

## 1. Business Overview

**Business Name:** Constitutional Platform Co.  
**Entity Type:** LLC (initially)  
**Mission:**  
To provide a platform that allows individuals and organizations
to form, govern, and evolve businesses using transparent,
human-centered governance and incentive systems.

The Company itself is the first adopter of its own framework.

---

## 2. Why This Model Is Used

The founders want to:
- start with an idea, not capital
- attract contributors without hiring immediately
- reward planning, coordination, and governance labor
- avoid premature employment or securities complexity
- allow people to join and leave without harm
- evolve into a corporation later if needed

The Constitutional Governance Framework is adopted for this purpose.

---

## 3. Initial Formation

### 3.1 Founders

- 1–2 founding Members
- Founders draft:
  - Operating Agreement
  - Governance Roles
  - Token Definitions
  - Distribution Policy

Founders initially hold:
- 100% equity
- Constitutional Authority
- Managing Authority

---

### 3.2 Allocation Directives (Constitutional)

At formation, the Company adopts the following Allocation Directives:

| Directive | Purpose | Allocation |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Platform Sustainability | Infrastructure, hosting, compliance | 30% |
| Contributor & Governance Rewards | Planning, stewardship, coordination | 40% |
| Founder Allocation | Founder compensation & risk | 20% |
| Future Planning & Reserves | Long-term initiatives | 10% |

These percentages apply **only if distributions occur**.

---

## 4. Token System (As Implemented)

The Company defines the following internal units:

### 4.1 Reward Tokens
- Issued for:
  - feature ideas
  - documentation
  - testing
  - design feedback
- Non-transferable
- No guaranteed value

---

### 4.2 Governance Reward Tokens
- Issued to:
  - stewards
  - planners
  - coordinators
- Recognize governance labor
- May convert to Allocation Credits under policy

---

### 4.3 Allocation Credits
- Issued sparingly
- Represent long-term participation in value distribution
- Snapshot-based
- Non-transferable
- Do not represent ownership

---

## 5. Early Participation Phase (No Payroll)

### 5.1 Contributors

The Company opens contribution via:
- documentation tasks
- design feedback
- architecture discussions
- example business creation

Contributors:
- sign the Contributor Agreement
- are not employees
- are not promised compensation
- may receive Reward Tokens

---

### 5.2 Governance Stewards

As activity grows, the founders appoint:

- Platform Steward
- Documentation Steward
- Governance Steward

Stewards:
- are Members or invited to become Members
- accept scoped, time-bound roles
- receive Governance Reward Tokens
- may leave at any time

---

## 6. Governance in Practice

### 6.1 Weekly Operations

Each week:
- stewards review open initiatives
- tasks are refined
- contributions are validated
- discretionary rewards are issued

No voting, no token-weighted governance.

---

### 6.2 Monthly Review

Each month:
- stewardship roles are reviewed
- governance load is adjusted
- token issuance is reviewed
- risks are assessed

Burnout prevention is prioritized.

---

## 7. First Revenue & Distribution Example

### 7.1 Revenue Event

The Company earns revenue from:
- early adopters using the platform
- consulting or onboarding support

Revenue is retained initially.

---

### 7.2 Distribution Decision

At a later point, Managing Authority approves a discretionary distribution.

Example:
- Total distributable amount: $10,000

Allocation Directives applied:

- Platform Sustainability (30%): $3,000
- Contributor & Governance Pool (40%): $4,000
- Founder Allocation (20%): $2,000
- Planning & Reserves (10%): $1,000

Within the Contributor & Governance pool:
- Allocation Credits determine individual splits
- Credits are snapshotted
- No one is guaranteed payment

---

## 8. Evolution Scenarios

### 8.1 New Members Join

High-trust contributors may:
- be invited to become Members
- receive small equity grants
- accept governance roles

Membership is optional and revocable.

---

### 8.2 Hiring Employees

When execution becomes required:
- roles convert to employment
- wages are paid
- tokens become supplemental only

Clear separation is maintained.

---

### 8.3 Conversion to Corporation

If outside capital is desired:
- the Company converts to a corporation
- equity is formalized
- token systems remain internal
- governance adapts but principles remain

---

## 9. What This Example Demonstrates

This example shows that:

- a company can start with an idea
- governance labor can be rewarded safely
- tokens can exist without becoming securities
- contributors can help without being exploited
- ownership can expand intentionally
- the system scales without redesign

The Company does not rely on:
- speculative token value
- passive income narratives
- automation without authority
- anonymous governance

---

## 10. Key Takeaways for Adopters

If you are adopting this framework:

- start simple
- keep authority human
- reward governance labor
- document decisions
- evolve deliberately
- escalate compliance intentionally

The framework exists to support people, not replace them.

---

## 11. Plain-English Summary

- The platform company uses its own rules
- People help because they want to
- Governance work is recognized
- Money only flows when approved
- Tokens don’t promise profit
- Authority remains human
- Growth is intentional

This is what a healthy early-stage company can look like.
