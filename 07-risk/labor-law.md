# Labor Law & Worker Classification Risk Analysis (U.S.-First)

This document analyzes labor-law risks arising from task-based contribution,
reward systems, and blockchain-assisted workflows.

It defines guardrails to prevent worker misclassification as the Company scales
across U.S. states and into other countries.

This is a living document and must evolve with jurisdictional expansion.

---

## 1. Purpose & Scope

This document is designed to:
- prevent employee vs. contractor misclassification
- separate incentives from wages
- protect contributors and the Company
- enable open contribution without employment implications
- scale across U.S. states and international jurisdictions

Primary focus:
- U.S. federal labor law
- State-level variations (e.g., CA, NY)
- Task-based / gig-style contributions
- Incentive tokens and non-cash rewards

This document does not replace legal counsel.

---

## 2. Core Labor-Law Principles (U.S.)

U.S. labor law focuses on **economic reality**, not labels.

Key regulators:
- U.S. Department of Labor (DOL)
- IRS (worker classification)
- State labor agencies

Primary tests:
- DOL Economic Reality Test
- IRS Common Law Test
- ABC Test (certain states)

---

## 3. Key Risk Vector: Task-Based Systems

Task-based contribution systems become labor violations if they:
- substitute for wages
- imply guaranteed compensation
- create control/dependence relationships
- function as piece-rate labor

The Company’s system is designed to avoid these outcomes.

---

## 4. Worker Categories (Explicit)

The Company recognizes four distinct participation categories.

### 4.1 Employees
- Hired via employment agreement
- Paid wages or salary
- Subject to labor protections
- Rewards are **supplemental**, never substitutive

Employees must never be compensated solely via tokens or credits.

---

### 4.2 Independent Contractors
- Engaged via contract
- Paid per agreement
- Maintain independence
- May receive rewards **in addition** to contract payment

Contracts must:
- define scope
- define compensation
- disclaim employment relationship

---

### 4.3 Volunteers / Open Contributors
- No expectation of compensation
- Participation is discretionary
- No guaranteed rewards
- Rewards are symbolic or discretionary

This category is highest risk if not carefully bounded.

---

### 4.4 Members / Owners
- Participate as owners
- May contribute work
- Are not employees by default
- Compensation rules must be explicit

Ownership alone does not justify unpaid labor.

---

## 5. Guardrails Against Misclassification

### 5.1 No Wage Substitution
Rewards:
- must not replace wages
- must not be the primary income source
- must not be predictable or required

If work must be done → it must be paid via wage or contract.

---

### 5.2 No Required Hours
Participants must not:
- be required to work fixed hours
- be penalized for not participating
- be scheduled like employees

Participation is opt-in.

---

### 5.3 No Control Over Means & Methods
Avoid:
- dictating how work is done
- mandatory tools or processes
- ongoing supervision for contributors

Control indicates employment.

---

### 5.4 No Dependency
Avoid:
- long-term reliance on a single contributor
- exclusivity
- restricting outside work

Dependency indicates employment.

---

## 6. Task System Design Constraints

To remain compliant:

Tasks must:
- be optional
- be non-exclusive
- allow multiple submissions
- allow zero rewards
- be limited in scope
- not be core operational labor unless contracted

If a task is mission-critical → it requires a contract or employee.

---

## 7. Reward Tokens & Labor Law

### 7.1 Why Rewards Are Dangerous If Misused

Rewards become wages if they are:
- expected
- required
- predictable
- necessary for livelihood

---

### 7.2 Required Reward Characteristics

Rewards must be:
- discretionary
- non-guaranteed
- non-cash by default
- non-transferable
- revocable

Language must avoid:
- “pay”
- “compensation”
- “earnings”
- “rate”

Use:
- “reward”
- “recognition”
- “incentive”
- “bonus (discretionary)”

---

## 8. State-Level Considerations (U.S.)

### 8.1 California (AB-5 / ABC Test)
High risk state.

Presumptions:
- Workers are employees unless proven otherwise

Mitigations:
- Avoid using open contributors for core business functions
- Use contracts or employment for ongoing work
- Keep reward-only participation clearly optional

---

### 8.2 New York
Focus on:
- control
- economic dependence
- continuity

Mitigations:
- short-lived tasks
- no exclusivity
- no recurring expectations

---

### 8.3 Multi-State Strategy
The Company must:
- track participant location
- apply the strictest applicable standard
- restrict tasks by jurisdiction if needed

---

## 9. International Expansion (Forward-Looking)

When expanding internationally:
- treat each country as a new compliance zone
- do not assume U.S. logic applies
- expect stricter worker protections in EU, UK, Canada

Recommended approach:
- disable open tasks in new jurisdictions
- re-enable only after local review

---

## 10. Evolution Triggers (Mandatory Review)

The following require immediate labor-law review:

- tasks become recurring or ongoing
- contributors rely on rewards for income
- tasks replace paid roles
- rewards become predictable
- contributors are managed or supervised
- contributors are required to meet deadlines

If triggered:
→ Pause tasks  
→ Reclassify work  
→ Engage counsel  

---

## 11. Documentation Requirements

For compliance, maintain:
- task definitions
- reward policies
- contributor disclaimers
- contracts (where applicable)
- audit logs
- jurisdiction mapping

Documentation is the primary defense.

---

## 12. Anti-Patterns (Explicitly Prohibited)

- “Earn tokens by working X hours”
- “Guaranteed reward for task completion”
- “Required participation”
- “Core operations via open contributors”
- “Token-only compensation”

These create wage liability.

---

## 13. Governance Accountability

- Managing Authority owns labor compliance
- Stewards must flag risk escalation
- Technical systems must enforce optionality
- No system may auto-issue rewards without approval

---

## 14. Summary

The Company remains compliant by ensuring:

- work that must be done is paid
- work that is optional is truly optional
- rewards are recognition, not wages
- authority is human, not algorithmic
- jurisdictional differences are respected

Labor compliance is not static.
It must evolve with scale, geography, and usage patterns.
